# Q1 — Span of control: cell size and tree depth defaults

# Team-size (“Span”): Small is usually better.  Human studies (military, psychology, org science) converge on teams of only a few people.  For deep collaboration the sweet spot is ~4–6 (Hackman found an “ideal” ~4.6 ([www.questworks.io](https://www.questworks.io/blog/right-team-size-research.html#:~:text=People%20preferred%20teams%20of%204,maintain%20engagement%2C%20clarity%2C%20and%20accountability))), with diminishing returns above 8–10 ([www.questworks.io](https://www.questworks.io/blog/right-team-size-research.html#:~:text=Research%20from%20Dunbar%20,the%20value%20of%20additional%20perspectives)) ([www.questworks.io](https://www.questworks.io/blog/right-team-size-research.html#:~:text=People%20preferred%20teams%20of%204,maintain%20engagement%2C%20clarity%2C%20and%20accountability)).  Amazon’s famous “two-pizza” rule similarly caps teams at roughly 5–10 people ([fasterthannormal.co](https://fasterthannormal.co/mental-models/span-of-control#:~:text=team%20should%20be%20larger%20than,insight%3A%20the%20problem%20isn%27t%20that)).  Dunbar’s social‐brain layers (5‐15‐50‐150) imply only ~5 close collaborators ([www.questworks.io](https://www.questworks.io/blog/right-team-size-research.html#:~:text=British%20anthropologist%20Robin%20Dunbar%20proposed,Dunbar%2C%201992)).  Even militaries use tiny units: for example, a US fireteam is 4–5 soldiers, a rifle squad ~8–12 (two 4-man teams plus leader), and a platoon ~30 ([www.globalsecurity.org](https://www.globalsecurity.org/military/agency/army/squad.htm#:~:text=ImageSquads%2C%20sections%2C%20and%20crews%20generally,of%20squads%2C%20sections%2C%20and%20crews)) ([en-academic.com](https://en-academic.com/dic.nsf/enwiki/112968/#:~:text=Fireteam%204%20NCO%20Squad%2FSection%208%E2%80%9313,Platoon%20leader%20Company%2080%E2%80%93225%20Captain%2FMajor)).  In practice industry and academic aggregation find ~4–6 per tightly interactive team, 6–8 per moderately autonomous pod, and a hard cap ~10 ([www.questworks.io](https://www.questworks.io/blog/right-team-size-research.html#:~:text=Research%20from%20Dunbar%20,the%20value%20of%20additional%20perspectives)) ([www.questworks.io](https://www.questworks.io/blog/right-team-size-research.html#:~:text=People%20preferred%20teams%20of%204,maintain%20engagement%2C%20clarity%2C%20and%20accountability)).  

These limits **guard against coordination overload**.  Communication paths grow combinatorially (a 6-person team has 15 two-way links; 10 people have 45; 15 people 105 ([fasterthannormal.co](https://fasterthannormal.co/mental-models/span-of-control#:~:text=not%20about%20span%20of%20control,of%20group%20communication%20are%20unforgiving))), so larger teams spend most time syncing rather than producing.  The classic Ringelmann/social-loafing effect shows individual effort dropping as team size grows ([www.questworks.io](https://www.questworks.io/blog/right-team-size-research.html#:~:text=In%20the%20early%201900s%2C%20French,BVOP%20Journal)).  Empirically, teams beyond ~10 typically fragment or lose engagement ([www.questworks.io](https://www.questworks.io/blog/right-team-size-research.html#:~:text=People%20preferred%20teams%20of%204,maintain%20engagement%2C%20clarity%2C%20and%20accountability)).  For example, a startup that promoted one engineer to manage 12 others found he lost all coding context – spending 100% of time in 1:1s and unable to name key project risks ([fasterthannormal.co](https://fasterthannormal.co/mental-models/span-of-control#:~:text=You%27re%20seeing%20Span%20of%20Control,manages%20process%20instead%20of%20outcomes)).  In short, **5–8 per manager** is a practical target; beyond that the “one-hub” manager bottleneck produces a coordination storm ([fasterthannormal.co](https://fasterthannormal.co/mental-models/span-of-control#:~:text=not%20about%20span%20of%20control,of%20group%20communication%20are%20unforgiving)) ([fasterthannormal.co](https://fasterthannormal.co/mental-models/span-of-control#:~:text=You%27re%20seeing%20Span%20of%20Control,manages%20process%20instead%20of%20outcomes)).  

# Layers vs Span: Trade-offs.  When should you **add another layer** instead of widening a manager’s span?  The rule-of-thumb (TeamChart) is ~3–4 layers in a 50–200 person org ([teamchart.io](https://teamchart.io/org-design/layers-and-depth/#:~:text=Quick%20benchmark%3A%20most%20organizations%20with,overhead%20and%20slows%20decision%20speed)).  Each extra layer adds cost (≈10–15% overhead and decision latency ([teamchart.io](https://teamchart.io/org-design/layers-and-depth/#:~:text=Quick%20benchmark%3A%20most%20organizations%20with,overhead%20and%20slows%20decision%20speed))).  **Too flat (wide spans)**: one leader becomes a chokepoint.  E.g. a manager with >10 reports spends all day in meetings and misses tactical detail ([fasterthannormal.co](https://fasterthannormal.co/mental-models/span-of-control#:~:text=You%27re%20seeing%20Span%20of%20Control,manages%20process%20instead%20of%20outcomes)); quality suffers (Microsoft found >10:S developers under one manager saw higher defect rates ([jellyfish.co](https://jellyfish.co/blog/manager-to-engineer-ratio/#:~:text=A%20study%20by%20Microsoft%20in,engineers%20disengaged%20not%20only%20from))).  In a wide-flat org, many cross-contacts can trigger “coordination storms” and one-hub decision bottlenecks.  **Too deep (many levels)**: information filters through each layer (“telephone game” distortion ([teamchart.io](https://teamchart.io/org-design/layers-and-depth/#:~:text=Every%20management%20layer%20adds%20a,is%20valuable%20or%20just%20expensive))), slowing response.  TeamChart warns that multi-hop chains delay frontline signals: what should take hours can take days ([teamchart.io](https://teamchart.io/org-design/layers-and-depth/#:~:text=,can%20act%20on%20them%20because)).  As another sign, executives find that even healthy 5–8 spans become ineffective once 5–6 levels of hierarchy accumulate ([fasterthannormal.co](https://fasterthannormal.co/mental-models/span-of-control#:~:text=When%20restructuring%2C%20use%20span%20of,produces%20better%20outcomes%20than%20layoffs)) ([teamchart.io](https://teamchart.io/org-design/layers-and-depth/#:~:text=,can%20act%20on%20them%20because)).  In practice, if managers are already near ~8–10 directs, it’s better to split the team (adding a mid manager) than faking a flat structure.  Conversely, if many tiny teams exist, merging them can reduce needless layers.  Collapse “pass-through” layers: if a director simply rubber-stamps a manager’s proposals ([teamchart.io](https://teamchart.io/org-design/layers-and-depth/#:~:text=,can%20act%20on%20them%20because)), that layer can be removed to speed decisions.  

# Industry norms (“EM:IC ratios”).  Real-world tech companies have settled around modest spans.  Recent surveys show **1 manager : 6–9 engineers** is common ([jellyfish.co](https://jellyfish.co/blog/manager-to-engineer-ratio/#:~:text=jeopardize%20long,every%20six%20to%20nine%20engineers)) ([www.vereda.ai](https://www.vereda.ai/guides/engineering-manager-direct-reports#:~:text=,2%20years)).  For example, Jellyfish (2025) reports Google, Microsoft, Amazon cluster at ~1:6–9 (Netflix ~1:5–6, Meta occasionally ~1:12) ([jellyfish.co](https://jellyfish.co/blog/manager-to-engineer-ratio/#:~:text=jeopardize%20long,every%20six%20to%20nine%20engineers)).  Vereda’s engineering survey similarly found averages ~7 (ideal ~7) with 5–9 being most effective ([www.vereda.ai](https://www.vereda.ai/guides/engineering-manager-direct-reports#:~:text=,2%20years)).  New/small teams often use even smaller spans (3–5) to ensure management time ([www.vereda.ai](https://www.vereda.ai/guides/engineering-manager-direct-reports#:~:text=%2A%20Effective%20range%3A%205,2%20years)).  These ratios *survive in practice* because they balance attention vs autonomy: ~1:7 allows some coaching time per person, while anything much higher shortchanges development.  By contrast, **cargo-culted rules** fail: rigidly enforcing 2-pizza (6-7) arbitrarily can fragment work, and “no managers” styles (Valve/37Signals) can founder at scale.  Experience matters: senior, autonomous teams can handle larger spans (Google found mature groups managing ~15 each ([tomtunguz.com](https://tomtunguz.com/span-of-control/#:~:text=responsibility%20books,%E2%80%9D))), whereas new or complex projects demand tighter spans.  Importantly, too low a ratio breeds micromanagement, too high leaves engineers without support ([jellyfish.co](https://jellyfish.co/blog/manager-to-engineer-ratio/#:~:text=Engineering%20teams%20suffer%20when%20the,challenges%20their%20team%20might%20face)). 

# Failure modes prevented.  In summary, keep teams around 4–8 members (max ~10) to **avoid communication overhead and social loafing ([fasterthannormal.co](https://fasterthannormal.co/mental-models/span-of-control#:~:text=not%20about%20span%20of%20control,of%20group%20communication%20are%20unforgiving)) ([www.questworks.io](https://www.questworks.io/blog/right-team-size-research.html#:~:text=People%20preferred%20teams%20of%204,maintain%20engagement%2C%20clarity%2C%20and%20accountability))**.  Cap each manager’s span similarly (roughly 1:6–9 for humans ([jellyfish.co](https://jellyfish.co/blog/manager-to-engineer-ratio/#:~:text=jeopardize%20long,every%20six%20to%20nine%20engineers))) to ensure quality attention (e.g. code review, mentoring ([www.vereda.ai](https://www.vereda.ai/guides/engineering-manager-direct-reports#:~:text=))) and avoid burnout (mgr. overload was linked to higher defect rates ([jellyfish.co](https://jellyfish.co/blog/manager-to-engineer-ratio/#:~:text=A%20study%20by%20Microsoft%20in,engineers%20disengaged%20not%20only%20from))).  Limit layers to a few levels (≈3–5 deep) so that signals aren’t lost – each additional level adds delay ([teamchart.io](https://teamchart.io/org-design/layers-and-depth/#:~:text=Every%20management%20layer%20adds%20a,is%20valuable%20or%20just%20expensive)) ([teamchart.io](https://teamchart.io/org-design/layers-and-depth/#:~:text=,can%20act%20on%20them%20because)).  This guards against the classic “bottleneck vs telephone” trade-off: a very flat hierarchy bottlenecks at one person, whereas a very tall one distorts and delays messages.  When spans or layers get out of balance, restructure (split teams or collapse layers) rather than simply adding headcount ([fasterthannormal.co](https://fasterthannormal.co/mental-models/span-of-control#:~:text=When%20restructuring%2C%20use%20span%20of,produces%20better%20outcomes%20than%20layoffs)) ([teamchart.io](https://teamchart.io/org-design/layers-and-depth/#:~:text=Quick%20benchmark%3A%20most%20organizations%20with,overhead%20and%20slows%20decision%20speed)).  

# “What to steal” for AI agent orgs.  Autonomy and cheap comm suggest some relaxations, but attention/compute is still finite.  Practical steps: 
- **Small modular teams**: Keep “cells” to ~5–10 agents for tightly-coupled work ([www.questworks.io](https://www.questworks.io/blog/right-team-size-research.html#:~:text=Research%20from%20Dunbar%20,the%20value%20of%20additional%20perspectives)) ([www.questworks.io](https://www.questworks.io/blog/right-team-size-research.html#:~:text=People%20preferred%20teams%20of%204,maintain%20engagement%2C%20clarity%2C%20and%20accountability)). AI agents can talk fast, but an individual agent’s processing (attention) is still limited.  
- **Single-threaded leads**: Each cell has one lead agent controlling a specific output (à la AWS microservices) ([fasterthannormal.co](https://fasterthannormal.co/mental-models/span-of-control#:~:text=coordination%20costs%20at%20the%20team,of%20group%20communication%20are%20unforgiving)) ([fasterthannormal.co](https://fasterthannormal.co/mental-models/span-of-control#:~:text=not%20about%20span%20of%20control,of%20group%20communication%20are%20unforgiving)).  This minimizes cross-talk overload.  
- **Wider but watched spans**: An AI “manager” could perhaps handle ~10–15 subordinates (more than a human), but watch for overload.  Monitor “span usage” (who spends how much CPU on coordination) – if it spikes it’s time to split the team.  
- **Flat-by-default**: Exploit cheap comm to avoid unnecessary middle layers.  Aim for few levels (e.g. 2–3) in even a large org.  If information flow lags (checksums slow, pipeline latencies rise), add a layer or reassign roles.  
- **Alignment boundaries**: Organize teams around clear tasks or services (like microservices) so inter-team chat is limited.  Agents then communicate via well-defined protocols rather than ad-hoc chats.  
- **Adaptive structuring**: Periodically re-evaluate structure.  If an agent’s “calendar” is full of coordination (like the 12-report manager in ([fasterthannormal.co](https://fasterthannormal.co/mental-models/span-of-control#:~:text=You%27re%20seeing%20Span%20of%20Control,manages%20process%20instead%20of%20outcomes))), reassign reports. Use metrics of “overhead time” to decide when to delayer or splitter.  
- **Reject one-size rules**: Don’t dogmatically copy any single number.  Instead, tune by complexity: give high-capacity agents larger spans, retrench around novices.  Emulate the principle behind Bezos’ rule – team sizes set for communication efficiency ([fasterthannormal.co](https://fasterthannormal.co/mental-models/span-of-control#:~:text=team%20should%20be%20larger%20than,insight%3A%20the%20problem%20isn%27t%20that)), not arbitrary HR policy.  

By blending these lessons, an AI organization can stay nimble (small teams, few layers) without falling into the traps of human orgs (overburdened leads or endless red‐tape layers). Sources above give concrete targets (5–9 members or reports) and cautionary thresholds (≳10 is risky), so they can guide your agent hierarchies while letting cheap network bandwidth do the heavy lifting.
