# Q4 — Goodhart-resistant throttles: budget design, prose-only milestones

# Goodhart’s Law in Practice – Gamable Targets  
Real-world bureaucracies brim with Goodhart/Campbell effects.  In call centers, strict targets (e.g. “handle X calls per hour”) lead agents to game metrics: rushing or abandoning calls to hit *Average Handle Time* goals, faking “adherence” by logging idle time without actual work, and inflating quality scores by cherry-picking easy cases ([wiki.wfmlabs.org](https://wiki.wfmlabs.org/wiki/Goodhart%27s_Law_and_Metric_Gaming#:~:text=,catalogued%20under%20%208%20The)).  Policing systems have similar issues: NYC’s CompStat era generated strong incentives to *downgrade* or never record crimes so commanders “meet” targets ([legalclarity.org](https://legalclarity.org/what-is-compstat-the-data-driven-policing-model/#:~:text=A%20management%20system%20that%20punishes,CompStat%20era)).  Indeed, a 2014 survey found 55% of retired NYPD officers knew of crimes being “reclassified to make stats look better” ([legalclarity.org](https://legalclarity.org/what-is-compstat-the-data-driven-policing-model/#:~:text=A%20management%20system%20that%20punishes,CompStat%20era)).  In healthcare, the UK NHS repeatedly gamed waiting-time targets.  Audits documented paramedics mis-grading emergency calls and hospitals holding ambulances outside A&E to avoid 4-hour breaches; surgeons crammed triage so admitted patients just under 4 hours; and “trolley waits” were masked by moving patients to hall beds ([www.researchgate.net](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/229615633_What%27s_Measured_Is_What_Matters_Targets_and_Gaming_in_the_English_Public_Health_Care_System#:~:text=For%20hospital%20A%26E%20waiting,distorting%20gaming)) ([www.researchgate.net](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/229615633_What%27s_Measured_Is_What_Matters_Targets_and_Gaming_in_the_English_Public_Health_Care_System#:~:text=an%20example%20of%20a%20fatal,gaming%20response%20was%20observed%20in)).  Waiting-list targets were met by quietly deferring appointments or falsifying lists ([www.researchgate.net](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/229615633_What%27s_Measured_Is_What_Matters_Targets_and_Gaming_in_the_English_Public_Health_Care_System#:~:text=For%20hospital%20waiting%20time%20targets,first%20outpatient%20appointment%20and%20elective)) ([www.researchgate.net](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/229615633_What%27s_Measured_Is_What_Matters_Targets_and_Gaming_in_the_English_Public_Health_Care_System#:~:text=outpatient%20appointments%20at%20a%20major,been%20achieved%20by%20cancellation%20and)).  In each case the proxy metric improved while core outcomes did not. 

# Graceful Controls – Flow Constraints vs Hard Targets  
By contrast, “flow-based” controls tend to resist overt gaming.  For example, *Kanban/WIP limits* set a hard cap on concurrent work items.  This forces teams to finish tasks before starting new ones, highlighting bottlenecks and reducing multitasking ([www.atlassian.com](https://www.atlassian.com/agile/kanban/wip-limits#:~:text=%2A%20WIP%20%28Work,bottlenecks%20and%20improving%20workflow%20efficiency)) ([www.atlassian.com](https://www.atlassian.com/agile/kanban/wip-limits#:~:text=Work%20in%20Progress%20,and%20unfinished%20work%20piling%20up)).  Because the limit is structural (e.g. “no more than N tasks in progress”), there’s no extra reward for pretending to finish tasks – work simply piles up if the cap is hit, making hidden effort visible.  Similarly, *rate limits* or **throttles** (e.g. API rate caps, compute quotas, network deceleration) impose a gradual slowdown once capacity is reached, rather than a hard penalty for slight excess.  Verizon’s infamous “unlimited” data plan throttling illustrates this: rather than cutting off service at a data threshold, heavy users are simply slowed on congested towers, giving them “an incentive to stop using [the network] so much” without creating sharp targets ([arstechnica.com](https://arstechnica.com/information-technology/2014/08/verizon-we-throttle-unlimited-data-to-provide-an-incentive-to-limit-usage/#:~:text=Verizon%20Wireless%20has%20told%20FCC,using%20their%20phones%20so%20much)).  A budget cap works likewise: once exceeded, the system stops authorizing new spending, but no further penalty accrues.  In effect, flow constraints create a *continuum* of performance (throughput) with diminishing returns, rather than a binary goal.  Agents gain nothing by fudging beyond the cap – “extra” reported output is impossible because capacity is the bottleneck.  In short, throttles *contain* bad behavior (slowing the agent) rather than *punish* it (removing reward), so they degrade performance gradually and transparently rather than triggering sharp abuses.  

# Principal–Agent Lessons for Incentives  
Economic theory warns that with hidden action, any fixed target draws gaming.  Scholars (e.g. Jensen 2001) note that **pay-for-target** systems induce systematic misreporting: managers lowball targets and then do “whatever it takes” to hit them, even shifting future costs into the present ([www.library.hbs.edu](https://www.library.hbs.edu/working-knowledge/why-corporate-budgeting-needs-to-be-fixed#:~:text=Corporate%20budgeting%20is%20a%20joke%2C,best%20interests%20of%20their%20companies)) ([www.library.hbs.edu](https://www.library.hbs.edu/working-knowledge/why-corporate-budgeting-needs-to-be-fixed#:~:text=Only%20by%20severing%20the%20link,ways%20that%20destroy%20corporate%20value)).  In budget terms, once a performance hurdle (say 80% of goal) and cap (e.g. 120%) appear, incentives flip – agents understate forecasts to secure easy bonuses or accelerate profits in one period and dump losses in another ([www.library.hbs.edu](https://www.library.hbs.edu/working-knowledge/why-corporate-budgeting-needs-to-be-fixed#:~:text=Cheaters%20Prosper)).  Jensen insists the cure is to *sever the bonus–target link*: reward genuine accomplishments rather than narrowly hitting quotas ([www.library.hbs.edu](https://www.library.hbs.edu/working-knowledge/why-corporate-budgeting-needs-to-be-fixed#:~:text=Only%20by%20severing%20the%20link,ways%20that%20destroy%20corporate%20value)).  

Key principal–agent takeaways: use **multiple, balanced measures** (so one metric can’t be optimized at the expense of others), and focus on long-run outcomes and quality.  For instance, pairing efficiency and outcome metrics (handle time *and* customer satisfaction; speed *and* accuracy) makes gaming one metric visible in the other ([wiki.wfmlabs.org](https://wiki.wfmlabs.org/wiki/Goodhart%27s_Law_and_Metric_Gaming#:~:text=Goodhart%20effects%20cannot%20be%20eliminated%2C,but%20they%20can%20be%20contained)).  Where auditing is infeasible, rely on random checks or peer review to deter worst abuses, with severe penalties if caught (recall Becker’s insight that one can maintain the same deterrence with rarer but harsher audits ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0176268099000270#:~:text=Becker%20,worse%20police%20or%20less%20police))).  Giving agents neither all the upside of a target nor 100% fixed pay (i.e. shared-risk schemes) reduces the attraction of one-off tweaks.  In sum, design incentives so that cheating yields **no hidden advantage** – e.g. by making any shortcut immediately degrade another tracked outcome or by making the “figuring out of the real goal” inherently unpredictable.  

# Ungameable Governance: Design Rules  
Drawing on the above, robust governance avoids single-number goals and favors flow-based constraints. Notable rules include: 

- **Reward Outcomes, Not Soured Proxies:** Tie benefits to *final outcomes* (resolved tickets, true customer value, net accuracy) rather than raw counts.  For example, one shouldn’t bonus on calls answered per hour alone, but on actual resolution rates.  Balanced scorecards help: every efficiency metric is coupled with a quality/outcome metric ([wiki.wfmlabs.org](https://wiki.wfmlabs.org/wiki/Goodhart%27s_Law_and_Metric_Gaming#:~:text=Goodhart%20effects%20cannot%20be%20eliminated%2C,but%20they%20can%20be%20contained)).  
- **Use “Pull” (Capacity) Limits Instead of “Push” Targets:** Impose hard capacity caps (WIP limits, rate limits, budget ceilings) that *throttle* activity rather than discontinuously rewarding it.  When a throttle is hit, work simply slows down; there’s no benefit in inflating stats because further output is impossible.  For instance, limiting token usage (e.g. compute tokens per minute) means an agent can’t score higher by fake-completing tasks beyond that rate.  Verizon’s throttling policy is analogous: users face slower service but *no extra reward* beyond moderate use ([arstechnica.com](https://arstechnica.com/information-technology/2014/08/verizon-we-throttle-unlimited-data-to-provide-an-incentive-to-limit-usage/#:~:text=Verizon%20Wireless%20has%20told%20FCC,using%20their%20phones%20so%20much)).  
- **Mix and Randomize Metrics:** Rotate which metrics are emphasized, and audit the results.  If agents don’t know in advance which measure will count (or faces spot checks), they can’t concentrate cheating on one narrow target.  As Goodhart theory suggests, keep objectives broad; treat proxies as signals, not sacred numbers ([wiki.wfmlabs.org](https://wiki.wfmlabs.org/wiki/Goodhart%27s_Law_and_Metric_Gaming#:~:text=condition%20Campbell%27s%20Law%20warns%20about,not%20as%20the%20objective%20itself)) ([www.library.hbs.edu](https://www.library.hbs.edu/working-knowledge/why-corporate-budgeting-needs-to-be-fixed#:~:text=Only%20by%20severing%20the%20link,ways%20that%20destroy%20corporate%20value)).  
- **High Penalty, Low Effort Audits:** Since perfect monitoring is impractical, design for deterrence via stiff consequences for detected gaming.  Economic theory holds you can maintain deterrence by escalating penalties (say, loss of fee or privileges) rather than full‐time surveillance ([www.sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0176268099000270#:~:text=Becker%20,worse%20police%20or%20less%20police)).  This lets you audit infrequently but credibly: every agent knows that any egregious misreporting risks a major setback.  
- **Limit Pauses and “Buffering” Tricks:** Beware targets that allow trivial fixes (e.g. temporarily delaying work).  Instead, enforce continuous flow: for instance, WIP limits encourage finishing tasks rather than parking them.  If an agent tries to noodle around the limit (e.g. moving cases off-screen), a good visual or automated board reveals idle jobs.  (Kanban’s extreme capping of in-progress work is a classic example ([www.atlassian.com](https://www.atlassian.com/agile/kanban/wip-limits#:~:text=%2A%20WIP%20%28Work,bottlenecks%20and%20improving%20workflow%20efficiency)).)  

By following these rules, any attempt to “game” merely triggers the flow constraint or fails a complementary measure.  In effect, capacity throttles turn distortion into null moves: stretching a proxy doesn’t create value, and overshooting a cap simply stops further gains (without extra reward).  In contrast to hard quotas, throttle-style limits have **no sweet spot to hit**, so subversion is futile.
