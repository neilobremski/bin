# Communicating Intent in Autonomous Organizations  

Humans use concise, purpose-driven **intent statements** to let teams act independently.  For example, U.S. military doctrine defines a *Commander’s Intent* as “a clear and concise expression of the purpose of the operation and the desired end state” ([studylib.net](https://studylib.net/doc/25858034/adp-6-0-mission-command#:~:text=1,Each%20commander%E2%80%99s%20intent)).  It is **not** a step-by-step plan or full concept of operations ([www.globalsecurity.org](https://www.globalsecurity.org/military/library/report/call/call_98-24_ch1.htm#:~:text=,their%20efforts%20toward%20that%20end)).  Instead, intent focuses on *what* must be achieved (mission and end-state) and *why*, empowering subordinates to decide *how*.  Jim Mattis notes that intent lets “people act” even under uncertainty, and it “demands a higher level of … discipline … than issuing voluminous, detailed instructions” ([pavilion.dinfos.edu](https://pavilion.dinfos.edu/Article/Article/2160695/commanders-intent-across-the-services/#:~:text=the%20challenge%20of%20communicating%20the,than%20issuing%20voluminous%2C%20detailed%20instructions)).  Well-written intent thus provides only the essentials (purpose, key details, success criteria) and explicitly leaves the “how” to the team ([pavilion.dinfos.edu](https://pavilion.dinfos.edu/Article/Article/2160695/commanders-intent-across-the-services/#:~:text=In%20drafting%20his%20intent%2C%20he,what%20you%20intend%20to%20do)) ([www.globalsecurity.org](https://www.globalsecurity.org/military/library/report/call/call_98-24_ch1.htm#:~:text=,their%20efforts%20toward%20that%20end)).  

In practice, military units use two-way briefs to cement understanding.  After an order is given, each subordinate conducts a **confirmation brief (backbrief)**: they summarize their understanding of the commander’s intent, their assigned tasks and purpose, and how they fit with other units ([www.globalsecurity.org](https://www.globalsecurity.org/military/library/report/call/call_98-5_rehears4.htm#:~:text=Confirmation%20Brief%20,leaders%20brief%20the%20commander%20on)).  This immediately surfaces mis-alignments.  Likewise, before finalizing plans, subordinates present a **backbrief** of *how* they intend to execute, so the commander can spot problems or misinterpretations early ([www.globalsecurity.org](https://www.globalsecurity.org/military/library/report/call/call_98-5_rehears4.htm#:~:text=Backbrief%20,allows%20the%20higher%20commander%20to)).  These exchanges ensure the succinct intent statement has been received correctly.  Under uncertainty, this pattern beats handing down detailed orders: rigid instructions quickly break when the situation changes, whereas a shared intent lets agents **adapt** to unfolding events while still pursuing the overall goal ([studylib.net](https://studylib.net/doc/25858034/adp-6-0-mission-command#:~:text=1,Each%20commander%E2%80%99s%20intent)) ([www.globalsecurity.org](https://www.globalsecurity.org/military/library/report/call/call_98-24_ch1.htm#:~:text=,their%20efforts%20toward%20that%20end)).  

## Basecamp Shape Up (Product Teams)  

In Basecamp’s **Shape Up** development process, authors adopt a related approach.  Instead of asking engineers for open-ended time estimates, managers set a fixed *“appetite”* (time budget) and shape the feature to fit ([basecamp.com](https://basecamp.com/shapeup#:~:text=Appetite%20The%20amount%20of%20time,which%20pitches%20to%20bet%20on)).  Pitches – written project proposals – define each upcoming cycle’s work.  A pitch typically states: the *problem* to solve, the *time appetite* (how long we’re willing to work on it), and a proposed *solution outline*.  Crucially, pitches also list “rabbit holes” or *no-gos* – things explicitly out of scope ([basecamp.com](https://basecamp.com/shapeup#:~:text=,55)).  Stakeholders review pitches at a “betting table” meeting and decide which to fund for the next cycle.  

Once a pitch is approved, teams get an uninterrupted cycle (about six weeks) to build it.  A strict **circuit breaker** rule applies: if the work doesn’t ship by the end of the cycle, the project is cancelled by default rather than extended ([basecamp.com](https://basecamp.com/shapeup#:~:text=Circuit%20breaker%20A%20risk%20management,Cleanup%20mode)).  This forces teams to trim scope instead of procrastinating.  In short, Shape Up encodes intent by fixing *what* will be done (the chosen pitch) and *when* (the appetite), without rigid task lists.  Teams re-read the pitch and timebox each session, adjusting implementation details themselves to meet the goal within the given appetite ([basecamp.com](https://basecamp.com/shapeup#:~:text=Appetite%20The%20amount%20of%20time,which%20pitches%20to%20bet%20on)) ([basecamp.com](https://basecamp.com/shapeup#:~:text=,55)).  This keeps the focus on outcome over micro-management.  

## Amazon Working Backwards & Handbook Culture  

Tech companies also codify intent via written documents.  Amazon’s **Working Backwards** process starts with a short, customer-facing press release (PR) and FAQ before any code is written ([www.aboutamazon.com](https://www.aboutamazon.com/news/workplace/an-insider-look-at-amazons-culture-and-processes#:~:text=Most%20of%20Amazon%27s%20major%20products,for%20press%20release%2Ffrequently%20asked%20questions)).  The PR/FAQ lays out the envisioned product and customer benefits in clear language, forcing clarity on *why* the feature matters.  (By design, the press release is under ~1 page and the FAQ under 5 pages ([www.aboutamazon.com](https://www.aboutamazon.com/news/workplace/an-insider-look-at-amazons-culture-and-processes#:~:text=Over%20time%2C%20we%20refined%20and,has%20come%20from%20that%20work)), to distill only the key ideas.)  Teams iteratively refine this document in reviews – often rewriting it many times – effectively making it a single source of truth for the project.  This written spec aligns all stakeholders on goals and constraints before work begins.  The handbook culture at companies like GitLab extends the idea: GitLab maintains an **open handbook** (>2000 pages) as the “central repository” for how the company runs ([handbook.gitlab.com](https://handbook.gitlab.com/handbook/#:~:text=The%20GitLab%20team%20handbook%20is,use%20issues%20to%20ask%20questions)).  Every process, policy and decision rationale is documented there (and updated by pull requests) so everyone refers to the same authoritative source.  In both cases, detailed living documents carry the organizational intent and keep teams coordinated.  

## OKRs and Cascading Goals  

Objective-Key-Result (OKR) frameworks illustrate pitfalls of mis-encoded intent.  When high-level OKRs are **mechanically cascaded** down the hierarchy, many organizations stumble.  Analysts note that waiting for each layer’s goals creates a “wait state” that stalls teams (“if the CEO is two weeks late, the entire company is frozen” ([performspark.ai](https://performspark.ai/blogs/okr-failure-rate-cascading-vs-alignment#:~:text=The%20Speed%20Trap))).  Additional alignment meetings, spreadsheets and tools add heavy overhead ([www.mindtheproduct.com](https://www.mindtheproduct.com/cascading-okrs-more-trouble-than-its-worth/#:~:text=1,long)) ([www.mindtheproduct.com](https://www.mindtheproduct.com/cascading-okrs-more-trouble-than-its-worth/#:~:text=3,software)).  Worse, strictly top-down OKRs can ignore ground-level insights – engineers or salespeople often have real-time knowledge that a peeled plan misses ([performspark.ai](https://performspark.ai/blogs/okr-failure-rate-cascading-vs-alignment#:~:text=The%20Information%20Gap)) ([www.mindtheproduct.com](https://www.mindtheproduct.com/cascading-okrs-more-trouble-than-its-worth/#:~:text=1,long)).  In practice, cascading OKRs too rigidly tends to cause misalignment and frustration.  Experts now suggest instead that leaders *publish strategic themes* and let teams independently set goals that support them, updating in a continuous (not waterfall) cycle ([performspark.ai](https://performspark.ai/blogs/okr-failure-rate-cascading-vs-alignment#:~:text=The%20Information%20Gap)) ([www.mindtheproduct.com](https://www.mindtheproduct.com/cascading-okrs-more-trouble-than-its-worth/#:~:text=3,software)).  The lesson: intent signals should guide and inspire teams, not lock them into a stale hierarchy of tasks.  

## Designing a Short Intent Document  

Drawing these lessons together, a concise **human-authored intent document** (for AI agents to re-read) should include: 

- **Purpose and End-State:** A brief mission statement of *why* and *what* success looks like ([studylib.net](https://studylib.net/doc/25858034/adp-6-0-mission-command#:~:text=1,Each%20commander%E2%80%99s%20intent)) ([www.nwcg.gov](https://www.nwcg.gov/wfldp/toolbox/briefing-intent#:~:text=conducted%20for%20a%20group%20or,they%20must%20do%20and%20why)).  This tells agents the goal and the desired outcome.  
- **Key Objectives/Outcomes:** Any critical results or objectives needed to consider the mission successful.  Ideally in measurable or observable terms.  
- **Scope and Constraints:** Clear boundaries – what is *in scope* and (importantly) *out of scope*.  List any prohibitions or “no-go” areas (as in Shape Up pitches ([basecamp.com](https://basecamp.com/shapeup#:~:text=,55))) so agents don’t wander into irrelevant work.  
- **Time/Resource Budget (“Appetite”):** If there is a fixed cycle length or resource cap, state it.  This helps agents trade off features vs. deadlines like in Basecamp’s timeboxed cycles ([basecamp.com](https://basecamp.com/shapeup#:~:text=Appetite%20The%20amount%20of%20time,which%20pitches%20to%20bet%20on)).  
- **Assumptions/Context:** Brief notes on key assumptions, environment, or supporting strategy that agents should keep in mind.  

Everything in the intent doc should be *high-level* and outcome-focused.  **Exclude** any detailed task list, implementation plan or step-by-step instructions ([www.globalsecurity.org](https://www.globalsecurity.org/military/library/report/call/call_98-24_ch1.htm#:~:text=,their%20efforts%20toward%20that%20end)).  The “how” is left to the agents (as in role of subordinates in mission command ([pavilion.dinfos.edu](https://pavilion.dinfos.edu/Article/Article/2160695/commanders-intent-across-the-services/#:~:text=In%20drafting%20his%20intent%2C%20he,what%20you%20intend%20to%20do))); over-specifying tasks will bog down autonomy.  The document should be very concise (ideally no more than a page or so) – sources stress that intent must be easily understood and memorable ([www.globalsecurity.org](https://www.globalsecurity.org/military/library/report/call/call_98-24_ch1.htm#:~:text=,their%20efforts%20toward%20that%20end)).  

The intent document should be reviewed at the start of each work cycle or when milestones change.  It remains largely *static during execution* so agents always refer to the same goal (in Shape Up each 6-week cycle has one intent set ([basecamp.com](https://basecamp.com/shapeup#:~:text=interruptions%20and%20an%20expectation%20to,on%20in%20the%20next%20cycle))).  Update it only at planned intervals (e.g. sprint or quarterly reviews) or if the mission itself shifts, to avoid confusion from constantly shifting goals.  

**Failure modes:** A stale intent (never updated) can mislead agents if the world changes.  Likewise, an over-spec’d intent (too many details) becomes a hidden micromanagement order, killing initiative ([www.globalsecurity.org](https://www.globalsecurity.org/military/library/report/call/call_98-24_ch1.htm#:~:text=,their%20efforts%20toward%20that%20end)).  If the document is too vague, agents may drift with no clear target.  In sum, the intent doc must strike a balance: only the essential mission-level guidance in clear terms.  When succinctly framed and regularly revisited, such an intent enables autonomous agents to self-organize and still march toward the human-defined milestone.
