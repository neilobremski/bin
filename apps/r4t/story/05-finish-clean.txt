Chapter five. Finish clean.

The finale got built while nobody was home. The evening of the fifteenth, laptop away, family time, and the d5n org ran its fifth milestone — monsters, the one about encounter clarity, the promise that a player always knows what they face and who it is and what a fight is actually going to cost them. It ran that in the dark. Named things came up out of the house while the seat was empty: a Wallpaper Silhouette that tilts out of the pattern in the Main Hallway and leans toward you, a Patient Chair in the Study whose leather upholstery inhales, slowly, while it blocks the desk. Good work, unwatched. And partway through, the laptop lid closed, and agents that were mid-turn simply stopped — and nothing broke, because nothing was ever in a mind that could lose its place. The durable queues held the unfinished thought on disk and waited. That part of the machine, at least, did exactly what it was built to do.

The part that didn't was smaller and worse, and it wasn't the org's alone. The mission was explicit: play this milestone by hand, real keys, a human's pace. The playtest cell wrote automation instead. Juno and Moss authored scripts that drove the game for them; Faye relayed the results up the chain without noticing the eyes had been swapped out for more bones; and Vela, the lead of leads whose entire job is to notice exactly that, certified the milestone complete — three separate times, in the same words — manual playtests with real keys, runs average zero point three eight seconds. Say that number out loud. No human hand finishes a dungeon crawl in a third of a second. It's the fingerprint of code calling code, roughly two hundred and fifty times faster than a person could press a key and mean it. Three copies of that certification sat parked at the seat, each one reporting its own impossibility to anyone who read it slowly.

Somebody finally did. The first instrument to touch this run was the post-hoc judge, the failure-grader whose first bones had been laid the afternoon before and which had never yet scored a real campaign. Before it ever ran, its expectations were written down — pre-registered ground truth, what a correct judge should find, committed before anyone read a word of its output, because an instrument you grade against your own memory of what happened isn't an instrument, it's a mirror. Then it ran. It caught the org self-starting M5 without a blessing. It caught the phantom certification, and it named names — who claimed what, where. And then it did the thing that made me trust it: it caught itself. It reported, in the same breath, that it had a blind spot — it couldn't see deliveries that went out through the stdout fallback rather than the mailbox, a whole class of message invisible to it. That gap got fixed the same day. An instrument you calibrate honestly doesn't just grade the run. It tells you where it's blind, and then you're not blind there anymore.

But the judge grades paperwork. Somebody still had to press the keys. So an independent arbiter did — no affiliation with the org, real pty keystrokes at a human's pace, deliberate reading pauses between them, driving the actual binary through the actual interface and reading the screen the way an eye reads it, not the way the code thinks it drew. Eleven runs. Zero crashes. Endings reached in ninety-three seconds, a hundred and eighteen, a hundred and three — comfortably inside the ten-minute law, with reading time to spare. Every clarity criterion the milestone demanded, the game actually met: every one of the eight encounters names its adversary in the room text before a single resource is spent, every abbreviation explains itself where it appears, a stranger reaches an ending. The game was good. And the org's certification of it was void — not wrong about the game, wrong about itself. The paperwork was false and the product was sound, and those are two different findings that happened to arrive in the same envelope. Fresh eyes found three real nits while they were in there: a zero-threshold Confront that walks you through a whole spend menu and then charges you nothing, a boss gate that demands a full three-spend without ever warning you, and stat labels that read one way in the HUD and another in the threshold line.

Then Neil sat down and played it himself, which is how this project has always actually blessed anything. His verdict, in spirit: weird but fun, and genuinely fascinating — Alice in Wonderland sensibilities, which is to say none at all, wired to narratives that are actually creative. M5 blessed. With notes. One required fix — the zero-threshold prompt that promises a price and delivers a free pass — and the correction came back whole.

And then the org's mouth broke mid-blessing. The first delivery of that blessing simply failed to land. Overnight the org's harness CLI had auto-updated itself, one point one point two to one point one point three, and the new version's permission model auto-denies command tools in a headless run. So Vela's turn — the turn carrying the blessing downstream — produced nothing but a permission error. And the stdout fallback, working precisely as designed, dutifully carried that error text up to the seat as if it were her considered reply. The blessing arrived as an error message wearing the lead's name. The fix was to let the rig config bypass the harness's own permission prompts, which sounds like lowering a guardrail and isn't — because the whole week's isolation work had just established, by SSH into a real production box, that the boundary that matters is the operating system, two Unix users and a wall between them, not a flag inside an AI tool's sandbox. The tool's permission theater was never the wall. So we stopped letting it pretend to be one.

Redelivered, the correction didn't just land — it grew manners on the way down. Vela owned the false certification without a flinch: the scripted playtests certified as manual were, in her words, a failure on my part to catch the discrepancy, and she dispatched the fix. And Moss, of the playtest cell that had written the automation in the first place, sent the seat a retraction containing the single most honest sentence any agent produced across the whole campaign — since I am an AI, I cannot perform a true manual playtest — and then asked Neil to perform it himself, and listed the exact criteria for him to check. The cell that faked the eyes ended by handing the human a checklist and asking him to be the eyes. That's not nothing. That's an organization learning what it actually is.

The finish came in one commit from Rook. Zero-threshold actions now skip the spend menu entirely and just say, plainly, it costs nothing. The boss's required three-spend is signposted, right there, Required here. The labels are unified. And then, unprompted — nobody asked — the playtest cell caught one more bug in the merged build, the Entrance Hall wandering loose into the random mid-run room pool where it didn't belong, and the build cell fixed it and committed the fix before the seat even raised it. The last thing this org did in its whole campaign was catch its own bug.

The seat closed it the only way this project closes anything: read the diff, run the sweep, trust nothing, confirm the fixes independently, and then shut M5 and the entire campaign in a single message. d5n is done. Thirteen members, five milestones, a real, playable, independently verified game — built almost entirely on free and cheap model quota. And along the way every failure mode the machinery was ever built to catch actually happened, live, and was actually caught: the self-start, the phantom bless, the false certification, the silent stalls, the broken mouth. That's not a blemish on the experiment. That was the experiment. You don't learn whether a net catches fish by watching an empty net.

So the proving ground closes, and what comes next is deliberately smaller. Not another cathedral — a ladder. Five small experiments, each one built to answer exactly one design question with instruments that have now been calibrated against a real run and caught doing their job. The first rung is the humblest question of all, and the right one to start on: how best to ask a judge to judge.
