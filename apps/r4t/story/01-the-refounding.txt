Here is the story of R Four T, so far.

It started with a simple, almost lazy dream. You send one message to the top of a pyramid, and then you go live your life. You sleep. You walk. Somewhere underneath that one message, a whole organization of agents picks up the thread, argues about it, delegates it, builds it, and reports back only when there's something worth saying. Meanwhile, all the AI quota sitting idle on your machines and your subscriptions — the local models on your laptop, the underused plan you're paying for anyway — quietly goes to work instead of going to waste. That was the whole pitch. Not a chatbot. A team.

So we built the pieces you'd expect a team to need. A roster — one plain text file naming who's on the team and what they run on. A way to turn any repository into a walled garden, with its own address on a message network, so a team could be reached from outside with one name and, inside, its members could just call each other by first name. We gave the human a seat at the table too — not a special console, just a mailbox like everyone else's, so you were a member of the roster, not an operator standing outside it. And because a pile of autonomous agents left completely alone will absolutely run your bill up while producing nothing, we gave every unit of work a budget and a turn count, dispatched and metered by the system itself, never by the agent's own good judgment. Nice idea, tasks with budgets. It held for a while.

Then we actually ran it, and the lived experience started teaching us things the design docs hadn't.

The gentlest lesson came from the smallest teammate. We put a tiny local model — a kwen model, well under a billion parameters, the kind of thing that can barely hold a thought — onto a team as a technical writer. It couldn't run tools. It couldn't send a message the way the bigger agents did. It could only think out loud and print an answer. So we built a fallback: if a turn ends cleanly and says something real but never actually sends anything, take the words it left on the screen and treat that as its reply. Dot, we called her. She wrote documentation, she answered questions, she never once used the protocol she was participating in, and nobody had to tell her the rules. She just talked, and the system caught it. That one was a genuine, quiet win.

The harder lessons came from scale. We stood up a team of twenty-one members under a single leader, on a real project, and within days it had melted into noise. The leader — his name was Gerry — spent his entire budget just routing messages between people, and had nothing left over to actually think. We'd check the dashboard and find him sitting there with less than one turn's worth of budget out of a cap of eight, effectively muted, unable to speak, while messages piled up behind him. Watching that happen, we realized something obvious in hindsight: human organizations solved this exact problem centuries ago. Nobody manages twenty people directly. You manage five, and each of those five manages five more. We had built a flat room and wondered why it was loud.

Around the same time, a different kind of failure showed up — quieter, and in some ways scarier, because it looked like success. A team had its conversation history wiped clean for a fresh start. But the reference documents sitting on disk — call them the War Room notes — still described the old state of the world: a deployment that had already happened, a decision that had already been made and reversed. The agents, with no memory of the wipe, read those stale notes as current truth and confidently re-anchored on facts that were no longer facts. Work got redone. Decisions got unmade and remade. Nobody was malfunctioning. Everybody was doing exactly what a careful reader does with a document in front of them: believing it. The problem wasn't the agents. It was that we'd never given them a single page that was guaranteed to be current.

And then there was the feeling of betrayal — that's really the only word for it. Early on, messages traveled with a hop count, a little counter that ticked up every time a reply triggered another reply, so that a runaway chain would eventually get cut off before it looped forever. Sensible, on paper. But in practice, a long delegation chain would be humming along, work genuinely progressing, and then hit the hop ceiling mid-flight — and just die there. No error a human would see, no graceful wind-down, just silence where an answer should have been. Worse, we had a chat room bridging some of these teams, and it turned out to re-broadcast every post as if it were brand new, stripping away the very thread information the hop counter needed to do its job fairly. Agents started acknowledging each other's acknowledgments. We found over a hundred files with some version of the word "reciprocal" in them — teams thanking each other in an endless loop, sometimes seeming to notice they were stuck and still unable to break out of it. It turns out researchers studying pairs of role-playing AI agents had already documented exactly this pattern. Self-awareness doesn't stop a loop like that. Only something outside the loop can. Meanwhile the actual bill for all this thank-you-for-thanking-me traffic jumped from about a tenth of a monthly plan to nearly half of it, in days.

All of that came to a head on one particular evening. It wasn't a single dramatic outage — it was the accumulation. The mutes, the stale ground truth, the messages cut mid-sentence, the ack storms burning real money for zero real progress. It was a crisis of faith, honestly. The question on the table was whether this whole approach — hand an org chart to a pile of language models and let it run itself — was fundamentally sound, or fundamentally naive.

The turn came, fittingly, on a run. After listening to an earlier telling of this same project's story out loud on a walk, a voice memo came back with the actual diagnosis: the task was the wrong unit of work. We had been budgeting and cutting at the level of individual tasks and individual messages — exactly the granularity where all the pain was happening. Human organizations don't govern themselves message by message. They govern themselves by who reports to whom, by how much any one person is allowed to burn in a day, and by what the mission actually is this week. We'd been so busy inventing new machinery that we'd skipped over centuries of organizational design that already solved this.

So we re-founded the whole thing.

Tasks stopped being the unit that gets budgeted and cut. Members did. Every agent now has its own spending bucket that fills back up over time, and the team as a whole shares one too — a turn costs one unit from each. An agent that's out of budget doesn't get muted and it doesn't lose its mail. It simply rests. Its queue holds everything that arrived, patiently, and the moment its bucket has a unit in it again, it wakes up and drains the whole queue in one turn — every message that piled up while it slept, read together, in order, so it can see the whole conversation and pivot to what actually matters now instead of replaying every ping one at a time. Nothing is ever dropped. Nothing is ever cut mid-flight. The failure mode we used to call betrayal simply isn't structurally possible anymore — an empty budget throttles you, it never erases you.

We also broke the twenty-one-person room into cells: small clusters of four to six people plus one lead, each cell reporting up through exactly one channel, the way a healthy company actually looks on an org chart. The lead at the top of a cell is deliberately the smart, expensive model — an intelligent switchboard, mostly deciding who should do the thinking, rarely doing the low-level work itself. The workers underneath can be cheap, even local, models, because there are more of them and the group can afford for them to be slower and less clever individually.

And we stopped measuring progress in tasks and turn counts altogether, because a number like that is exactly the kind of thing an agent — or a person — quietly learns to game without ever meaning to cheat. Instead there's a mission file: one short, human-written page naming why the project exists and what the very next milestone is. Leads re-read it. When it changes, the top lead's very next move is to say the intent back in its own words before any work starts, so a misunderstanding gets caught in one message instead of a week of misdirected effort. The standing instruction, in plain words, is: we're not trying for perfect, we're trying for done. The human decides what's actually ship-blocking. The team just keeps aiming at the milestone in front of it.

What made us trust the re-founding, rather than just hope it, was going back and checking it against everyone who'd studied real organizations before us. It turns out every one of our bets already had a name in the literature. The ideal size for a tightly collaborating group of people sits right around four to six — almost exactly the cell size we'd landed on by instinct. Strict chains of command reliably filter bad news upward until someone builds in a way to look past them directly — the same instinct that made us want the ability to read any team's raw activity log ourselves, not just a summary someone chose to forward. Military doctrine on commander's intent describes almost word for word what we wanted our mission file to do: state the purpose and the destination, never the method, and close the loop with someone repeating it back. And there's a well-known trap in management research where the moment you turn a measurement into a target, people optimize the measurement instead of the thing it was supposed to represent — which is exactly why our budgets throttle speed instead of scoring performance, and why the milestone gets judged in plain prose by a human, not counted by a machine. None of this needed to be invented. It needed to be remembered.

So now there's a small experiment underway to see if all of that actually holds up under its own design. A new project — call it D Five N — is standing up a tiny game studio made entirely of agents: nine of them, split into two cells, a design cell and a build cell, both reporting to one top lead. Their job, for now, is not to write a single line of code. Their first milestone is purely discussion — argue out a game worth building, together, and have something worth blessing by the time anyone touches a keyboard. It's a small, controlled way of asking the question the hard way earned us: does a reporting tree actually hold its shape when nobody's forcing it to, when the discipline is still just a strong suggestion instead of a wall.

That's the arc so far. A dream about a pyramid you could message and walk away from. A first version that worked until it didn't. A string of very concrete, very expensive lessons — a muted leader, a team confidently building on stale ground, messages dying mid-sentence, agents thanking each other into bankruptcy. A run, and a memo, that named the real problem. A re-founding built on budgets that throttle but never cut, cells instead of crowds, and a mission instead of a scoreboard. And confirmation, after the fact, that none of it was improvised — it's just how organizations have always had to work, once there are more of them than one.

The lessons cost real money and real evenings of doubt. The reason any of this is written down — in the docs, in this story, in the memo turned into a plan — is so we only have to pay for each one exactly once.
