Chapter two. The honest run.

Neil came home from the park and, before anyone told him a thing, sat down and walked the house himself. Arrow keys, Enter, no report standing in for the real experience. That was the blessing — not a message saying "M2 done," but a human at a keyboard doing what a report can never substitute for.

So M2 closed and M3 opened, with teeth this time: not "can you install it and take a few steps," but "one honest run" — real decisions, an ending you could actually reach, kept by the house or escaped from it, honestly inside ten minutes, not a rounded-up version of ten. A new cell joined to keep "honest" a fact and not a word: Faye as playtest lead, Juno the impatient stranger who mashes keys, Moss the completionist who reads every line. Their charter changed the team's incentives in one sentence — a finding from playtest outranks a claim from build.

Before any of that kicked off, Neil did two acts of hygiene that mattered more than they looked. First: the team must never learn it's an experiment. Call it the Hawthorne effect — knowing you're being studied changes how you behave, agent or human. So the lab notebook moved out of the team's own repo to a folder they never see, the roster line calling this an experiment with the game as pretext got scrubbed, and Nib, the smallest voice on the build cell, stopped being labeled a "realism probe" and became, in the roster's own words, just a junior scribe — same job, none of the framing. Second: every agent's memory was wiped clean, no history of the run that just happened. Not a bug — the ground-truth system working as designed. Wipe the memory, and all that's left to re-anchor on is what's written down: the blessed design, the mission file. A clean mind converges on truth faster than a cluttered one, provided the truth is on disk.

Then the walls went up for real. Phase two of the reporting tree landed, changing what agents could conceive of, not just what they were allowed to do. Turn prompts now show only the names on your own branch — your lead, your reports, your cell-mates. Vela, lead of leads, cannot see the workers two levels below her, and anything aimed off-tree gets mechanically rerouted through the proper channel. Lateral chatter didn't become forbidden; it became unthinkable — there was no name on the screen to send it to.

The very first M3 kickoff produced a specimen worth keeping. Vela wrote a careful briefback in full prose, restating the mission's intent exactly as the ritual demands — then sent Neil one line: "briefback above — did I get it?" Except there is no "above" in a tell; the message is the whole message, and nothing written ahead of it in her own head crosses the wall. One correction later she resent the entire briefback in-band, word for word, no worse for it — the mistake a sharp new hire makes on day two, having learned the ritual but not yet which parts live in her head and which have to travel.

For a stretch after that, Faye went quiet — genuinely quiet, early turns with nothing sent. Pulled against the transcript, the silence turned out to be discipline, not failure: "Standing by. Nothing to test until the build lands." A playtest lead who won't manufacture acknowledgment spam to look busy is doing exactly the job. Underneath that, a smaller, funnier discovery: opencode had quietly seated Faye on a free-tier deepseek model nobody asked for, because that's what was available and unspent. Nothing goes to waste on this team.

While Faye waited, the rest of the team closed a whole quality loop on its own. Cass ruled the mechanics — thresholds, corridors against vaults, the boss, how a run ends. Rook built exactly that. Faye, once the build landed, did something nobody taught her to do: wrote her own headless test harnesses and ran ten playtests through them, finding two render bugs and a boss-fight ordering problem — the code checked for death before it checked for a win, so a winning push could resolve as a loss. Cass ruled win-check-first, Rook fixed it, all three outcomes verified.

Then the independent check caught what the in-house loop structurally could not. With Neil out, an Opus proxy playtester stood in and played roughly fourteen runs with real key presses through the real interface — not calling handlers directly the way Faye's harness had to. The game went keyboard-dead after the first room. Faye's ten headless runs never saw it, because her harness drove the logic straight through, skipping the exact layer — focus, rendering, real input — where the bug lived. The interface and the logic are different truths, and only one is what a stranger actually experiences. The verdict came back BLESS-WITH-NOTES: sound underneath, the copy surviving contact with an independent reader — a death line that held up beautifully: "You are furniture now. The wallpaper settles around you like a blanket. It was never angry. It was only lonely."

The findings went back through the seat, and Vela triaged all three in one turn — the focus bug to Rook, a design question about the entrance greeting to Cass, who kept the Entrance Hall canonical, plus a note about commit discipline that turned out prophetic. The focus fix landed and got verified with real keys against a real running app — then sat uncommitted for the better part of an hour, the same lesson about stale ground truth wearing a different hat: a change on disk isn't yet a change that's recorded. One more nudge, and the commit finally landed for good. M3 closed from the team's side of the wall.

Nib had a quiet, useful day through all of it — tasked twice by Rook, answering both times through the stdout fallback, no tool calls, just words the system catches and treats as a reply. He wasn't needed for the final push, and that's fine; not everyone has to carry the ending. A bare-ollama rig preset now exists too — a way to seat a member on a local model with no tool harness at all, nothing to fail, nothing to misuse.

Worth pausing on what the day cost. The budgets bottomed out at least once and got retuned with a single knob change — the kind of fix that used to mean reinventing a subsystem. A "tempo" idea is queued for later: one ratio knob plus one overall speed knob, so a team moves faster without anyone re-deriving the budget math by hand. The whole afternoon, from Neil walking the house to a verified, committed fix, cost next to nothing, since the workers ran on local models and free-tier quota the entire time — idle compute spent instead of idle money. Every fix and every ruling got written down where it will be read again, so the team pays for each lesson exactly once.

Here is where the day rests. Two things wait entirely on Neil, and neither is something the machine can resolve on its own. There's a design ruling still open: the shipped loop is spend-only — pick an approach, spend health or willpower against it, see what happens — while the design's original junctions and lanes, choosing your path before you spend anything, were never actually built. Does spend-only satisfy "one honest run," or does it need the navigation too? And there's the blessing run itself, waiting the way M2's did. The house stands installed, scripted, played by a stranger with real keys, ruled on by everyone whose job it is to rule — everything an organization can do without a player in the chair. The only move left is the one no amount of delegation can perform: someone has to sit down, play the game, and decide. The house waits for it, politely, the way it always does.
